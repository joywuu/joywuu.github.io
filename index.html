System Design for climate Resilience: Future of Seafood

Massachusetts boasts the second largest fishery in the U.S., thanks to the Gulf of Maine’s abundant fishy residents. Surprisingly, 70 - 85% of the seafood eaten in the U.S. is imported, with classics like shrimp, salmon, tuna, and our state-famous cod (yes, there’s a giant wood cod in our state house!).

Our marine ecosystem’s production is diverse and dynamic. Fishermen’s daily catch is constantly influenced by the ecosystem, especially under the changing climate. For example, black sea bass landings in MA are at all-time high in part due to warming waters pushing these fish north.

How might we bring more climate winner seafood to local markets?

Now, let’s explore “new” - the lesser-known and emerging - local species to better align with our ocean and support responsible practices that ensure a future with diverse, healthy, and delicious seafood options. Next time your recipe calls for Atlantic cod, try substituting with other more plentiful mild, flakey fish, like Atlantic pollock.
